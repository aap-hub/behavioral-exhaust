# 2. Related Work

Our work sits at the intersection of four research threads: empirical analysis of coding agent trajectories, uncertainty quantification in language models, chain-of-thought faithfulness, and SWE-bench evaluation methodology. We position our contribution relative to each.

## 2.1 Coding Agent Trajectory Analysis

Several concurrent studies analyze what makes coding agents succeed or fail on SWE-bench, but they focus on action-level patterns --- what the agent *does* --- rather than reasoning-level content --- what the agent *says before acting*.

Majgaonkar et al. (2025) study trajectories from OpenHands, SWE-agent, and Prometheus, finding that failed trajectories are longer, more variable, and exhibit distinct problem-solving strategies. Their analysis operates on action sequences: file reads, edits, searches. We analyze the reasoning text that precedes these actions, a layer their work does not address. Our finding that failing tasks produce 38% more tool calls is consistent with their trajectory-length result, but we show that *per-call* reasoning properties (not trajectory-level aggregates) carry the discriminative signal once the length confound is controlled.

Zhang et al. (2026a) propose Holistic Trajectory Calibration (HTC), extracting process-level features --- trajectory length, action diversity, token distributions --- to build interpretable calibration models across eight benchmarks. HTC achieves cross-domain transferability, a property our linguistic features lack. However, HTC does not analyze reasoning text content, nor does it distinguish between internal thinking and external messages. Our thinking/message dissociation demonstrates that collapsing these layers discards --- or inverts --- signal.

Cuadron et al. (2025) take a selection approach with Shepherd, using LLM-as-judge to identify optimal trajectories among multiple completions, improving o1-low from 21% to 31% on SWE-bench. Their discriminator is a learned model; our features are lightweight regex-based extractions. The two approaches are complementary: behavioral features could serve as interpretable inputs to a system like Shepherd's, reducing the number of candidate trajectories that require expensive LLM evaluation.

Bouzenia and Pradel (2025) analyze thought-action-result coherence in 120 trajectories from three repair agents, identifying behavioral motifs and anti-patterns. Their semantic coherence analysis is closest in spirit to our reasoning-to-action alignment metric, though operationalized differently. Their premature-actions anti-pattern partially overlaps with our first-edit-position feature.

## 2.2 Uncertainty Quantification in LLMs

The question of whether language models can express calibrated uncertainty has generated a large literature. Kadavath et al. (2022) show that P(True) estimates are reasonably calibrated for factual questions, but this approach requires API access to logit distributions --- unavailable in our setting (OAuth-only authentication, no logprob access). Xiong et al. (2024) survey verbalized uncertainty methods and find them broadly unreliable for complex reasoning. Zhang et al. (2026b) propose Agentic Uncertainty Quantification, transforming verbalized uncertainty into active control signals via dual-process memory and reflection. Their system *uses* verbalized uncertainty as input; our study *evaluates whether* it carries signal in the first place.

Xuan et al. (2026) study miscalibration in tool-use agents specifically, finding systematic overconfidence. Our Hyland hedging null result provides complementary evidence: coding agents do not express uncertainty through the lexical markers that work in question-answering, suggesting that miscalibration in this domain is not merely a matter of degree but of kind.

## 2.3 Lexical Signals in Reasoning Chains

Vanhoyweghen et al. (2025) report that lexical markers of uncertainty --- "guess," "stuck," "hard" --- are the strongest indicators of incorrect responses in QA reasoning chains, achieving F1 = 0.83. They find that Hyland-style hedging words and CoT length are also informative for moderate-difficulty benchmarks. Our work provides a direct domain contrast. The same Hyland hedging vocabulary that carries signal in QA is completely non-predictive for coding (rho near zero). What works instead are structural features: how much the agent deliberates per call, not whether it hedges. This divergence is consistent with Vanhoyweghen et al.'s own finding that domain-specific markers outperform generic ones, but we push it further: in the coding domain, no purely lexical uncertainty marker we tested carries signal. The informative features are structural (deliberation length, error patterns) or domain-grounded (diagnostic precision, code-reference density).

## 2.4 Chain-of-Thought Faithfulness

A growing body of work establishes that chain-of-thought content does not always reflect a model's internal reasoning process. Arcuschin et al. (2025) find that CoT is unfaithful in extended thinking, particularly for Claude 3.7 Sonnet. Lanham et al. (2023) develop paraphrasing and early-answering tests for faithfulness measurement. Chen et al. (2025) report that unfaithful CoTs are systematically *longer* than faithful ones --- a structural signal embedded in unfaithful content.

Our thinking/message dissociation extends this line of work in two ways. First, we show that the same lexical feature can carry opposite polarity depending on which layer it is extracted from: "actually" in thinking blocks signals genuine confusion (negative association with success), while "actually" in agent messages signals performative correction (positive association). This is not merely unfaithfulness --- it is a *structured* divergence where each layer independently carries useful but directionally opposite information. Second, our findings are in the coding domain, where prior faithfulness work has focused on mathematical reasoning and factual QA. The practical implication is that behavioral feature extraction systems must specify which layer they operate on; collapsing thinking and messages produces noise or, worse, cancellation.

Bachmann et al. (2026) introduce a "potential" measure quantifying how much each part of a CoT increases the likelihood of correct completion, discovering non-monotonic dynamics with sharp spikes. Our trajectory-position degradation finding is conceptually related: behavioral features are predictive in the early and middle trajectory but not the late trajectory. The mechanisms likely differ --- Bachmann et al. study single-pass mathematical reasoning; we study multi-turn agentic interaction --- but both establish that signal quality within reasoning traces is position-dependent.

## 2.5 SWE-bench Methodology

Our statistical approach draws on Anthropic's published methodology for model evaluations (Anthropic, 2025), which recommends clustered standard errors, paired tests, and power analysis for benchmark comparisons. We adopt their within-cluster philosophy through our CWC methodology: Fisher-combined within-repository Spearman correlations with within-repository permutation validation. Martinez and Franch (2025) provide a taxonomy of SWE-bench approaches, noting that most submitted systems do not exhibit truly agentic behavior; our study uses two genuinely agentic systems (Claude Code and Codex CLI) that make autonomous multi-step decisions. Bai et al. (2025) analyze token consumption patterns in coding agents, finding that resource usage varies dramatically across tasks --- a confound we control through per-call normalization of all linguistic features.
